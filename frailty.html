Chapter Overview

Chapter Number: 7
Last Updated: May 2026
Target Audience: Health professionals involved in cancer surgery prehabilitation programs, including surgeons, anaesthetists, nurses, allied health professionals, geriatricians, and general practitioners.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the reader will be able to:

  1. Define frailty and distinguish it from comorbidity and normal ageing
  2. Identify which patients require preoperative frailty screening
  3. Apply validated frailty screening tools including the Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) and Edmonton Frail Scale (EFS)
  4. Understand the role and components of the Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA)
  5. Identify modifiable frailty components and implement targeted prehabilitation interventions
  6. Recognise when specialist geriatric, allied health, and multidisciplinary referrals are required
  7. Apply strategies to mitigate the risk of postoperative delirium and cognitive dysfunction
  8. Facilitate shared decision making with frail patients and their families regarding surgical risk
  9. Implement quality improvement measures to evaluate frailty management programs

⚡ Key Clinical Recommendations

Critical Actions

  • •Screen all patients ≥65 years and any individual with reduced biological, physical, or psychosocial reserves
  • •Use a validated screening tool — Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) or Edmonton Frail Scale (EFS)
  • •Refer for Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA) all patients identified as frail on screening
  • •Identify modifiable frailty components — target exercise, nutrition, and psychological health via prehabilitation (COACH services)
  • •Begin discharge planning preoperatively for frail patients — social supports and disposition planning
  • •Commence delirium prevention strategies preoperatively — address polypharmacy (≥5 medications)
  • •Initiate shared decision making early when surgery is being contemplated

1. Background and Clinical Context

Frailty is a complex, multidimensional syndrome characterised by a decline of physical, sensory and/or cognitive reserves — or an accumulation of deficits — that leads to a reduced ability to withstand even relatively minor physiological insults or stressors. In the context of cancer surgery, frailty represents a critical risk factor that requires systematic identification and targeted intervention during the prehabilitation period.

Frailty is independently associated with higher rates of postoperative complications, increased hospital length of stay, ICU admission, and mortality in patients undergoing major cancer surgery. It is a distinct entity from comorbidity, although the two are often related. While frailty is generally observed in the geriatric population (>65 years), it can be present in younger individuals due to functional and physiological limitations associated with cancer and its treatment.

Key Conceptual Distinctions

Frailty

Reduced physiological reserve and resistance to stressors. Multidimensional — physical, cognitive, psychosocial. Potentially modifiable.

Comorbidity

Concurrent medical conditions (e.g., diabetes, cardiac disease). Related to but distinct from frailty. May contribute to frailty.

Disability

Impaired ability to perform activities of daily living. May result from frailty. Not synonymous with frailty.

2. Epidemiology and Impact

2.1 Prevalence in Cancer Surgery Populations

Frailty is highly prevalent in cancer surgery populations. Cancer itself accelerates the frailty trajectory through nutritional depletion, systemic inflammation, reduced physical activity, and treatment-related toxicity. Rates vary by tumour type, patient age, and frailty definition used, but frailty is consistently identified as one of the most important predictors of adverse perioperative outcomes in older surgical oncology patients.

3. Patient Selection and Screening

Screening Indication

Frailty screening should be carried out on all patients over 65 years of age, and any individual with reduced biological, physical, or psychosocial reserves, regardless of age.

Populations Requiring Frailty Screening

Age-Based Criteria

  • All patients aged ≥65 years
  • Patients aged <65 with significant functional limitations
  • Patients with cancer-related weight loss or deconditioning

Clinical Indicators (Any Age)

  • Reduced mobility or functional independence
  • Cognitive impairment or dementia
  • Polypharmacy (≥5 medications)
  • Malnutrition or significant weight loss
  • Social isolation or limited support networks
  • History of falls
  • Sensory impairment (vision, hearing)

Timing of Screening

Frailty screening should be performed at the earliest opportunity in the prehabilitation pathway — ideally at initial surgical consultation or prehabilitation assessment, at least 4–6 weeks before planned surgery to allow time for intervention and optimisation.

4. Frailty Screening Tools

CFS

Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS)

Visit website ↗

The CFS is a judgement-based tool to screen for frailty and to broadly stratify degrees of physical and cognitive function. Scores range from 1 (very fit) to 9 (terminally ill). Higher CFS scores are significantly associated with prolonged postoperative stay, readmission, morbidity, and mortality in older patients undergoing cancer surgery.

CFS Score Descriptions

1 – Very Fit: Robust, active, energetic, motivated
2 – Fit: No active disease symptoms, less fit than 1
3 – Managing Well: Medical problems well controlled
4 – Vulnerable: Not dependent but slowed, symptoms limit activity
5 – Mildly Frail: Limited dependence on others for IADLs
6 – Moderately Frail: Help needed with ADLs and IADLs
7 – Severely Frail: Completely dependent for personal care
8 – Very Severely Frail: Completely dependent, approaching end of life
9 – Terminally Ill: Life expectancy <6 months
EFS

Edmonton Frail Scale (EFS)

Visit website ↗

The EFS is a multidimensional assessment tool utilising an 11-item questionnaire to analyse 9 domains of frailty: cognition, general health status, functional independence, social support, medication use, nutrition, mood, continence, and functional performance (via a Timed Up and Go test). Higher EFS scores are associated with greater risk of postoperative complications.

💡 Choosing Between CFS and EFS

Use CFS when:

  • Rapid screening is needed (<5 minutes)
  • Remote or telephone assessment required
  • Broad stratification is sufficient
  • Clinician has clinical knowledge of the patient

Use EFS when:

  • Multidimensional domain assessment required
  • Identifying specific modifiable frailty components
  • Guiding targeted prehabilitation referrals
  • Patient is aged ≥70 years

5. Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA)

Assessment should be carried out using a validated multidimensional frailty instrument. Referral to a geriatrician or frailty service for completion of the Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA) is recommended for all patients identified as frail on screening.

What is the CGA?

The CGA is a multidimensional, interdisciplinary diagnostic process to determine the medical, psychological, and functional capabilities of a frail elderly patient, to guide appropriate interventions to support the patient. It can help identify frail patients who have a significantly increased risk of severe complications after elective surgery for colorectal cancer.

⚠ Limitation of CGA

No CGA components were found to be predictors of 30-day or 90-day mortality in systematic review evidence. The CGA is most useful for identifying interventional targets and guiding care planning, rather than as a mortality risk predictor alone.

6. Prehabilitation Intervention for Frail Patients

🎯

PRECAST Project — Specially Relevant Section

This section is of central importance to the PRECAST project. When the PRECAST calculator identifies modifiable frailty features (particularly via SHAP analysis or high eFI score), clinicians should immediately consider COACH referral and targeted prehabilitation. The PRECAST risk profile directly informs which interventions are most likely to reduce surgical risk for each individual patient.

As with all patients, prehabilitation aims to improve functional capacity, nutritional status, and psychological health. Frail individuals require broader multidisciplinary input to assist with optimisation of comorbidities and implementation of social supports. Discharge disposition planning should begin during the preoperative period.

🏋️

Exercise

Tailored exercise program to improve strength, endurance, and functional capacity. Physiotherapy-led, progressive resistance and aerobic training.

🥗

Nutrition

Dietitian-led nutritional assessment and optimisation. Protein supplementation, oral nutritional supplements, and management of malnutrition.

🧠

Psychological Health

Clinical psychology input for anxiety, depression, and cognitive concerns. Cognitive prehabilitation strategies where appropriate.

COACH Service (Combined Outpatient Allied Health Service)

The COACH service provides combined exercise, nutrition, and clinical psychology support. When modifiable frailty components are identified (particularly via SHAP analysis or EFI screening), clinicians should:

  • Refer to COACH for exercise, nutrition, and/or clinical psychology (marked with asterisk in referral list below)
  • If services are not provided by COACH, discuss at the MDT and ask a clinician on the team to make the appropriate referral

8. Delirium Prevention and Cognitive Risk Management

Frail patients are at significantly elevated risk of postoperative delirium and cognitive dysfunction. Strategies to mitigate these risks should begin preoperatively.

Risk Factors for Postoperative Delirium

  • Advanced age (≥65 years)
  • Existing cognitive impairment or dementia
  • Frailty (CFS ≥4)
  • Polypharmacy (≥5 medications)
  • Sensory impairment (vision, hearing)
  • Dehydration and malnutrition
  • Sleep disturbance
  • Social isolation

Preoperative Prevention Strategies

  • Medication review: Address polypharmacy; deprescribe anticholinergics, benzodiazepines, opioids where possible
  • Cognitive prehabilitation: Engage cognitive stimulation activities
  • Sensory aids: Ensure hearing aids and glasses are available and functioning
  • Nutritional optimisation: Address malnutrition and dehydration
  • Exercise: Improve physical conditioning and mobility
  • Sleep hygiene: Assess and treat sleep disorders
  • Family engagement: Involve family/carers in planning and orientation

Polypharmacy (≥5 Medications)

Polypharmacy should be systematically addressed in all frail patients. Medication review should be conducted by the treating team or a clinical pharmacist, with deprescribing of inappropriate medications where clinically safe. This reduces delirium risk, falls risk, and medication-related adverse events in the perioperative period.

9. Shared Decision Making

Shared decision making (SDM) should begin when surgery is being contemplated, and should incorporate discussions to establish who will be involved in medical decision-making and support. For frail patients, SDM is particularly critical given the elevated surgical risk and the importance of aligning treatment goals with patient values and preferences.

Key SDM Conversations for Frail Patients

  • Realistic discussion of surgical risks given frailty status
  • Expected recovery trajectory and functional outcomes
  • Alternative treatment options (non-operative, palliative)
  • Goals of care and patient values
  • Advance care planning and medical decision-making
  • Who will provide support post-surgery (family, carers)
  • Discharge planning and rehabilitation expectations

Participants in SDM for Frail Patients

  • Patient (central to all decisions)
  • Family members / carers (as appropriate)
  • Surgeon
  • Anaesthetist
  • Geriatrician (for CGA findings)
  • Oncologist (for oncological context)
  • Nurse coordinator / prehabilitation nurse
  • Palliative care (where appropriate)

💡 Discharge Disposition Planning

Discharge planning should begin during the preoperative period for all frail patients. Early identification of discharge needs (rehabilitation facility, home care services, equipment) avoids delays in discharge and reduces preventable readmissions.

10. Quality Improvement and Evaluation

Epidemiological Metrics

  • Prevalence of frailty (CFS ≥4 or EFS ≥5)
  • Prevalence by tumour type and age group
  • Proportion with CFS ≥6 (high risk)

Process Metrics

  • Frailty screening rate (eligible patients)
  • CGA referral rate for frail patients
  • Prehabilitation uptake in frail patients
  • Polypharmacy review completion rate
  • SDM documentation rate

Outcome Metrics

  • Postoperative complication rate by CFS
  • Hospital length of stay
  • ICU admission rate
  • Postoperative delirium rate
  • 30-day readmission rate
  • Discharge disposition (home vs. facility)

11. Clinical Decision Pathway

1

Patient Identification

Identify all patients ≥65 years, and any patient with reduced biological, physical, or psychosocial reserves at prehabilitation assessment.

2

Frailty Screening

Apply validated screening tool: Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) for rapid stratification, or Edmonton Frail Scale (EFS) for multidimensional domain assessment. Aim to complete at least 4–6 weeks before surgery.

3

Interpret Screening Result

CFS 1–3 (Low risk): Standard prehabilitation pathway.
CFS 4–5 (Moderate risk): Enhanced prehabilitation; consider CGA referral.
CFS ≥6 (High risk): Mandatory CGA referral; multidisciplinary planning; SDM discussions.

4

Identify Modifiable Components

Review EFI or EFS domains to identify modifiable frailty components (exercise capacity, nutrition, psychological health, polypharmacy, sensory impairment, social supports). Use SHAP analysis where available.

5

Initiate Targeted Prehabilitation

Refer to COACH for exercise, nutrition, and/or clinical psychology. For components not covered by COACH, discuss at MDT and arrange appropriate specialist referrals (see referral list in Section 7).

6

Delirium Prevention

Conduct medication review; address polypharmacy (≥5 medications). Ensure sensory aids in place. Optimise nutrition, sleep, and cognitive engagement. Engage family/carers in preoperative planning.

7

Shared Decision Making

Initiate SDM conversations regarding surgical risk, recovery expectations, goals of care, and advance care planning. Establish who will be involved in medical decision-making and support.

8

Discharge Planning

Begin discharge disposition planning preoperatively. Identify rehabilitation needs, home care requirements, and equipment. Coordinate with social work and occupational therapy early.

9

Proceed to Surgery with Multidisciplinary Plan

Communicate frailty status and management plan to surgical, anaesthetic, and ward teams. Ensure perioperative delirium prevention protocols are activated. Confirm postoperative rehabilitation and discharge plan is in place.

12. Summary and Key Points

  1. Frailty is a complex multidimensional syndrome characterised by reduced physiological reserve, distinct from comorbidity and normal ageing.
  2. Frailty is independently associated with postoperative complications, prolonged hospital stay, ICU admission, and mortality in cancer surgery patients.
  3. Screen all patients ≥65 years and those with reduced reserves using the Clinical Frailty Scale (CFS) or Edmonton Frail Scale (EFS).
  4. CFS provides rapid stratification (Low: 1–3, Moderate: 4–5, High: ≥6); EFS provides multidimensional domain assessment to guide targeted intervention.
  5. Refer frail patients for Comprehensive Geriatric Assessment (CGA) by a geriatrician or frailty service to guide comprehensive management.
  6. Identify and target modifiable frailty components via COACH services (exercise, nutrition, clinical psychology) and specialist referrals as needed.
  7. Delirium prevention strategies must begin preoperatively — address polypharmacy, sensory impairment, nutrition, sleep, and cognitive engagement.
  8. Discharge disposition planning should begin during the preoperative period for all frail patients.
  9. Shared decision making is essential for frail patients — align surgical decision-making with patient values, goals of care, and realistic outcome expectations.
  10. A multidisciplinary team approach is required for optimal management of frail cancer surgery patients.

Implementation Checklist for Prehabilitation Programs

12a. The Electronic Frailty Index (eFI)

PRECAST Calculator — Core Input Measure

The Electronic Frailty Index (eFI) is a validated, data-driven tool that calculates a cumulative deficit score from routinely collected electronic health record (EHR) data. It is one of the core input features used by the PRECAST calculator to generate a patient's individualised surgical risk profile. Unlike clinical frailty scales that require direct assessment, the eFI is derived automatically from coded clinical data, making it scalable across large populations.

How the eFI is Calculated

The eFI score is computed as: Number of deficits present ÷ Total number of possible deficits

0–0.12

Fit

No or very few deficits

0.13–0.24

Mild Frailty

Some deficit accumulation

0.25–0.36

Moderate Frailty

Multiple deficits present

>0.36

Severe Frailty

High deficit accumulation

⚡ Clinical Tip — eFI in PRECAST

When the eFI is high, review the individual deficits contributing to the score. Some (e.g., anaemia, diabetes, weight loss, dyspnoea) are potentially modifiable through prehabilitation. The PRECAST SHAP output (see Section 12b below) will show which of these deficits are most influential for the individual patient's risk score — prioritise those for intervention.

12b. Understanding the PRECAST Risk Profile — SHAP Analysis

PRECAST Calculator — Interpreting the Output

The PRECAST calculator uses a machine-learning model to generate an individualised surgical risk score. To make this model transparent and clinically interpretable, it produces a SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) waterfall plot for each patient. This plot shows exactly which features are increasing or decreasing that patient's risk relative to the average patient.

How to Read a SHAP Waterfall Plot

🔴 Red Bars (Positive SHAP)

Features that increase this patient's risk above the average. Longer bars = greater impact. These are the priority targets for intervention.

🔵 Blue Bars (Negative SHAP)

Features that decrease this patient's risk below the average. These represent protective factors or absent risk factors.

📊 Bar Length

The longer the bar, the more that feature is contributing to (or protecting against) this individual patient's risk score.

Example SHAP Waterfall Plot — PRECAST Risk Profile

Illustrative example showing how the PRECAST calculator explains an individual patient's risk score. Features in red increase risk; features in blue are protective. Hover over bars for details.

⚡ Clinical Action — Using the SHAP Output

  1. Identify the top 3–5 red (risk-increasing) features for this patient.
  2. Determine which are modifiable (e.g., weight loss/anorexia → Nutrition; dyspnoea → Respiratory; falls → Physiotherapy; anaemia → Haematology).
  3. For modifiable features covered by COACH (Exercise, Nutrition, Psychology), refer directly to COACH.
  4. For all other modifiable features, raise at the Tuesday MDT and request appropriate specialist referral (see Section 7).
  5. Document which features were targeted and review at follow-up to assess improvement in risk score.
