Chapter Overview

Chapter Number: 5
Last Updated: May 2026
Target Audience: Health professionals involved in cancer surgery prehabilitation programs, including surgeons, anaesthetists, nurses, allied health professionals, and general practitioners.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the reader will be able to:

  1. Define anaemia using WHO criteria and understand its clinical significance in cancer surgery patients
  2. Identify which patients require preoperative anaemia screening
  3. Order and interpret appropriate investigations for anaemia and iron deficiency
  4. Distinguish between absolute and functional iron deficiency in cancer patients
  5. Prescribe appropriate treatment for iron deficiency anaemia, including timing and dosing
  6. Recognise when specialist haematology referral is indicated
  7. Implement quality improvement measures to evaluate anaemia management programs

⚡ Key Clinical Recommendations

Critical Actions

  • •Screen all patients undergoing cancer surgery with predicted blood loss ≥500 ml for anaemia
  • •Test for iron deficiency using serum ferritin and transferrin saturation (TSAT)
  • •Treat iron deficiency with intravenous iron (1 g ferric carboxymaltose or ferric derisomaltose)
  • •Optimal timing: Administer IV iron 4–6 weeks before surgery when possible
  • •Refer to haematology: Patients with B12/folate deficiency, unclear iron status, or religious objection to blood transfusion

Diagnostic Thresholds

WHO Anaemia Thresholds

Females: Hb <120 g/L

Males: Hb <130 g/L

Iron Deficiency Definitions

Absolute: Ferritin <30 μg/L (healthy)

Functional: Ferritin <100 μg/L OR TSAT <20% (cancer/inflammation)

1. Introduction and Clinical Context

Anaemia is a common and clinically significant condition in patients undergoing cancer surgery. The World Health Organization (WHO) defines anaemia as haemoglobin (Hb) concentration below 120 g/L in females and below 130 g/L in males.

Preoperative anaemia in cancer patients is independently associated with adverse surgical outcomes, including increased perioperative morbidity, prolonged hospital stay, and elevated mortality risk. While the evidence that correcting anaemia directly modifies these risks remains evolving, treatment of anaemia offers several important benefits:

  • Improved quality of life in cancer patients
  • Enhanced functional capacity during prehabilitation
  • Reduced risk of perioperative blood transfusion
  • Optimisation of physiological reserve before major surgery

International consensus guidelines, including the Frankfurt Consensus Conference and Enhanced Recovery After Surgery (ERAS) protocols, recommend systematic detection and treatment of anaemia before major cancer surgery.

2. Epidemiology and Impact

2.1 Prevalence in Cancer Populations

Anaemia is particularly prevalent in cancer surgery populations, with the highest rates observed in:

  • Colorectal cancer patients: High rates due to chronic gastrointestinal blood loss
  • Gynaecological oncology patients: Elevated prevalence secondary to menstrual and tumour-related bleeding
  • Upper gastrointestinal malignancies: Associated with nutritional deficiencies and chronic blood loss

3. Patient Selection and Screening

Screening Indication

All patients should be screened for anaemia prior to cancer surgery when the predicted potential blood loss is 500 ml or greater.

Procedures Requiring Screening

  • Colorectal resections
  • Hepatobiliary and pancreatic surgery
  • Oesophagogastric resections
  • Major gynaecological oncology procedures
  • Urological cancer surgery
  • Head and neck cancer surgery with major reconstruction

Timing of Screening

Screening should occur at the earliest opportunity, ideally at initial surgical consultation or prehabilitation assessment — minimum 4–6 weeks before planned surgery.

High-Risk Populations

  • Patients with visible bleeding (haematochezia, melaena, haematuria, menorrhagia)
  • Colorectal and gynaecological cancer patients
  • Patients with poor nutritional status or weight loss
  • Those with previous anaemia or iron deficiency
  • Patients on anticoagulation or antiplatelet therapy

⚠ Tests to Avoid

Serum iron alone is NOT recommended as a measure of iron status — it fluctuates with time of day, meals, and supplementation. TSAT (incorporating serum iron) is more useful but still requires clinical context.

When to Refer to Haematology

  • Low vitamin B12 or serum folate levels
  • Unclear iron status despite initial investigations
  • Patients with religious or other objection to blood transfusion (mandatory referral)
  • Suspected haematologic disorders

5. Interpretation of Iron Studies

Serum ferritin is the best measure of total body iron stores in healthy individuals. However, ferritin is also an acute phase reactant and may be falsely elevated in the presence of active malignancy, systemic inflammation, infection, or liver disease.

Absolute Iron Deficiency

Ferritin <30 μg/L (in otherwise healthy individuals)

Functional Iron Deficiency

Ferritin <100 μg/L OR TSAT <20% (in cancer/inflammatory states)

Clinical Interpretation Table

FerritinTSATInterpretationAction

30–100 μg/L<20%Functional iron deficiency (cancer/inflammation)Treat with IV iron
<100 μg/L≥20%Possible functional iron deficiencyConsider IV iron in context

≥100 μg/L≥20%Iron repleteInvestigate other causes of anaemia

6. Treatment Recommendations

In patients with active inflammation, including active cancer, intestinal absorption of iron is significantly impaired due to hepcidin-mediated blockade of iron transport. Consequently, intravenous (IV) iron formulations are strongly preferred over oral iron supplementation in the preoperative cancer population.

IV Iron Formulations and Dosing

FormulationBrand NameTypical Dose
Ferric carboxymaltoseFerinject®1000 mg (1 g)
Ferric derisomaltoseMonofer®1000 mg (1 g)

Optimal Timing

IV iron should be administered 4–6 weeks prior to surgery when possible. Benefits can be observed even with supplementation as close as 1 day prior to surgery, but earlier administration is preferred.

Adverse Effects and Complications

Side EffectFrequencyManagement
NauseaCommonUsually self-limiting; antiemetics if needed
HeadacheCommonSimple analgesia
Constipation / DiarrhoeaCommonUsually self-limiting; dietary advice
Skin staining (extravasation)RareEnsure secure IV access; may be permanent
HypophosphataemiaNotableCheck phosphate if worsening fatigue; oral/IV replacement

⛔ Contraindication

IV iron should not be prescribed during active infection. Defer treatment until infection is resolved.

Chemotherapy-relatedMedication historyUnlikely correctable preoperatively

Declining Blood Transfusion

Mandatory referral to laboratory haematology for comprehensive preoperative optimisation and blood conservation strategies.

Patients on Anticoagulation

Continue standard screening and treatment. IV iron can be safely administered in anticoagulated patients.

Neoadjuvant Chemotherapy

Screen before and after chemotherapy. Chemotherapy-induced anaemia may not be fully correctable preoperatively.

9. Clinical Decision Pathway

1

Patient Identification

Patient scheduled for cancer surgery with predicted blood loss ≥500 ml → Order screening blood tests.

2

Initial Screening

Order: Full Blood Examination (FBE), Serum ferritin, Transferrin saturation (TSAT).

3

Haemoglobin Assessment

Is the patient anaemic? (Females: Hb <120 g/L; Males: Hb <130 g/L). If YES → also order UEC, CRP, B12, Folate.

4

Iron Status Assessment

Iron deficiency criteria: Ferritin <100 μg/L OR TSAT <20%. If YES → prescribe IV iron. If NO → investigate other causes.

5

IV Iron Treatment

Ferric carboxymaltose (Ferinject®) 1000 mg OR Ferric derisomaltose (Monofer®) 1000 mg. Administer 4–6 weeks pre-op. Repeat FBE 2–4 weeks after infusion.

6

Investigate Alternative Causes

If iron replete but anaemic: B12/folate deficiency → Haematology; Renal impairment → Nephrology; Unclear → Haematology; Chronic disease → Document, optimise other aspects.

7

Special Circumstances

Patient declines blood transfusion → Mandatory haematology referral. Unclear iron status → Haematology referral.

10. Summary and Key Points

  1. Anaemia is common and clinically significant in cancer surgery patients, associated with adverse outcomes and reduced quality of life.
  2. Universal screening is recommended for all patients undergoing cancer surgery with predicted blood loss ≥500 ml.
  3. Iron deficiency is the most common and most treatable cause of anaemia in this population.
  4. Different diagnostic thresholds apply in cancer patients due to inflammatory effects on ferritin (functional iron deficiency: ferritin <100 μg/L or TSAT <20%).
  5. Intravenous iron is the treatment of choice in cancer patients, with 1 g ferric carboxymaltose or ferric derisomaltose administered 4–6 weeks before surgery.
  6. Modern IV iron is safe, with negligible anaphylaxis risk but awareness needed for hypophosphataemia and skin staining.
  7. Haematology referral is indicated for B12/folate deficiency, unclear iron status, and patients declining blood transfusion.
  8. Quality improvement evaluation should include epidemiological, process, and efficacy metrics.
