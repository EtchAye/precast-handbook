Chapter Overview

Chapter Number: 8
Topic: Nutritional Prehabilitation
Relevant Clinicians: Dietitians, Surgeons, Anaesthetists, Nurses, Allied Health
Source: Prep-4-Cancer Surgery Toolkit (Peter MacCallum Cancer Centre)

Malnutrition is one of the most prevalent and modifiable risk factors in patients undergoing cancer surgery. This chapter provides a structured approach to nutrition screening, assessment, diagnosis, and intervention within the prehabilitation pathway, drawing on ESPEN guidelines and the Prep-4-Cancer Surgery Toolkit.

Learning Objectives

After reading this chapter, the clinician should be able to:

  • ✓ Describe the prevalence and clinical consequences of malnutrition in cancer surgery patients
  • ✓ Select and apply an appropriate validated malnutrition screening tool (MST, MUST, or PG-SGA SF)
  • ✓ Understand the role of the PG-SGA in comprehensive nutrition assessment and malnutrition diagnosis
  • ✓ Apply the GLIM criteria to diagnose and classify malnutrition severity
  • ✓ Stratify patients into universal, targeted, or specialist nutrition care pathways
  • ✓ Outline evidence-based nutrition interventions including protein targets, ONS, and dietary counselling
  • ✓ Recognise and address sarcopenia and muscle wasting in the preoperative period
  • ✓ Apply perioperative nutrition principles including preoperative carbohydrate loading and ERAS protocols
  • ✓ Identify appropriate quality improvement metrics for nutrition prehabilitation programmes

Key Clinical Recommendations

Screen All Patients

All individuals referred into prehabilitation should be screened for malnutrition risk using a validated tool (MST preferred in Australia). Screening should occur at diagnosis and be repeated during treatment.

Dietitian Referral Threshold

MST ≥ 2, MUST ≥ 2, or PG-SGA SF > 5 indicates malnutrition risk — refer immediately to a dietitian for comprehensive nutrition assessment. Do not delay referral pending surgery date.

High-Risk Surgical Groups

All patients undergoing major GI or gynaecological cancer surgery should be referred to a dietitian regardless of screening score, due to the inherently high risk of malnutrition in these groups.

PG-SGA for Assessment

The scored PG-SGA is the internationally recommended tool for diagnosing malnutrition in cancer patients. It stratifies patients into PG-SGA A (well nourished), B (moderate/suspected malnutrition), or C (severe malnutrition).

1. Background & Rationale

Malnutrition is a common and clinically significant condition in patients undergoing cancer surgery. It is directly linked to increased risk of postoperative complications, prolonged hospital stay, impaired wound healing, reduced quality of life, and higher mortality. Despite this, malnutrition remains underdiagnosed and undertreated in the preoperative period.

Evidence-based nutrition guidelines from ESPEN (European Society for Clinical Nutrition and Metabolism) and the Clinical Oncology Society of Australia (COSA) recommend that all individuals with cancer be screened for malnutrition risk at diagnosis and throughout treatment, including the preoperative period. The aim of nutrition prehabilitation is to identify patients at nutritional risk, diagnose malnutrition, and implement targeted interventions to optimise nutritional status before surgery.

2. Epidemiology & Clinical Impact

3. Malnutrition Screening

Who Should Be Screened?

Evidence-based nutrition guidelines recommend all individuals with cancer be screened for malnutrition risk using a validated tool at diagnosis, and during and after cancer treatment, including surgery. For this reason, all individuals referred into the prehabilitation service should be screened for malnutrition.

Why Is Nutrition Screening Important?

Malnutrition is directly linked to increased risk of post-operative complications. The aim of nutrition screening is to identify if an individual is at risk of malnutrition. If deemed at risk, the patient should be referred to the dietitian for a comprehensive nutrition assessment and diagnosis of malnutrition.

Which Screening Tool to Use?

4. Screening Tools — Comparison

Malnutrition Screening Tool (MST)

Recommended first-line tool in Australia

2 questions · 2 min

A quick, two-question tool investigating recent unintentional weight loss and recent poor intake due to poor appetite. Creates a score of 0–5. A score of 2–5 indicates risk of malnutrition, requiring dietitian referral. Validated in cancer patients, reliable, and requires no equipment (height/weight not needed). The most commonly used nutrition screening tool in Australia.

Malnutrition Universal Screening Tool (MUST)

Validated in acute and community adults

3 questions · 3 min

A three-question tool incorporating current weight and height (to calculate BMI), percentage of recent unplanned weight loss, and an acute disease state score. Creates a score of 0–6. A score of 2–6 is considered at risk of malnutrition. Validated in acute and community adults (not specifically cancer patients).

Patient-Generated Subjective Global Assessment Short Form (PG-SGA SF)

Validated in cancer patients — patient self-completed

4 boxes · 5 min

The first page of the full PG-SGA, designed for patient self-completion. Consists of four boxes: (1) body weight (scored 0–5); (2) food intake (0–4); (3) symptoms affecting oral intake (0–23); and (4) activities and function (0–3). A total score of >5 indicates risk of malnutrition, requiring dietitian referral. Validated in cancer patients.

Choosing the Right Tool

MST — Best for:

Rapid triage, no equipment needed, Australian clinical settings, remote/telehealth screening, any clinician or patient self-report

MUST — Best for:

Community and acute settings where height/weight are available, general adult populations, when BMI data is needed

PG-SGA SF — Best for:

Cancer-specific screening, patient self-report prior to clinic visit, capturing symptom burden, bridging to full PG-SGA assessment

5. Comprehensive Nutrition Assessment

Who Can Assess?

Nutrition assessment must be completed by a qualified/accredited dietitian. It is distinct from screening and requires clinical expertise to diagnose malnutrition, determine severity and causes, and plan targeted interventions.

Who Should Receive Assessment?

  • All patients screened as 'at risk' of malnutrition
  • All patients undergoing major GI tract surgery
  • All patients with known or suspected cachexia or sarcopenia
  • All patients with high-risk cancer types (upper GI, gynaecological)

Comprehensive Nutrition Assessment Domains

📋 Dietary History

Food intake, appetite changes, dietary restrictions, supplement use

⚖️ Anthropometrics

Weight, height, BMI, weight history, rate of weight loss

🔬 Biochemistry

Albumin (reflects disease severity), CRP, FBC, micronutrients

💪 Physical Exam

Muscle mass, subcutaneous fat, oedema (nutrition-focused physical exam)

🏃 Functional Status

Handgrip strength, 6-minute walk test, 30-second sit-to-stand, ECOG

🩺 Clinical Factors

Nutrition impact symptoms, disease stage, treatment history, comorbidities

The PG-SGA — Gold Standard Assessment Tool

The scored Patient-Generated Subjective Global Assessment (PG-SGA) is internationally recognised as the preferred tool for diagnosing malnutrition in cancer patients. It has been validated for use in cancer patients and is endorsed by COSA and ESPEN.

⚡ Note on Biochemical Markers

Low albumin is a clear surgical risk factor; however, it reflects disease-associated catabolism and disease severity rather than undernutrition per se. Nutrition diagnosis should not be based on biochemical data alone, but can complement a thorough nutrition assessment.

6. Malnutrition Diagnosis & Care Stratification

The most important part of comprehensive assessment is diagnosing whether the individual is clinically malnourished and the severity of malnutrition. This allows stratification into the appropriate level of nutrition care.

GLIM Criteria for Malnutrition Diagnosis

The Global Leadership Initiative on Malnutrition (GLIM) criteria provide a standardised framework for diagnosing malnutrition. Diagnosis requires at least one phenotypic criterion AND one etiologic criterion:

Phenotypic Criteria (at least 1 required)Etiologic Criteria (at least 1 required)
Low BMI <20 kg/m² (if <70 years) or <22 kg/m² (if ≥70 years) Reduced food intake ≤50% of estimated energy requirement for >1 week, or any reduction for >2 weeks
Unintentional weight loss >5% within 6 months, or >10% beyond 6 months Presence of inflammation Acute disease/injury, or chronic disease-related (cancer, major surgery, organ failure)
Reduced muscle mass Low at ≥4 sites on nutrition-focused physical exam

Nutrition Care Stratification (PG-SGA Based)

PG-SGA A

Universal Care

Well nourished. No significant weight loss or reduced intake.

  • General healthy eating advice
  • Protein and energy guidance
  • Written resources provided
  • Reassess if status changes
PG-SGA B

Targeted Care

Moderate or suspected malnutrition. Some weight loss or reduced intake.

  • Individualised dietitian counselling
  • Oral nutritional supplements (ONS)
  • Symptom management strategies
  • Regular monitoring and review
PG-SGA C

Specialist Care

Severe malnutrition. Significant weight loss, muscle wasting, reduced intake.

  • Intensive dietitian-led intervention
  • Consider enteral/parenteral nutrition
  • MDT involvement (medical, surgical)
  • Consider delaying surgery if safe

7. Nutrition Intervention

Nutrition interventions in prehabilitation are guided by the PG-SGA stratification and delivered by the dietitian. The primary goals are to optimise energy and protein intake, address nutrition impact symptoms, and improve muscle mass and functional status before surgery.

Energy & Protein Targets

⚡ Energy Requirements

25–30 kcal/kg/day for most cancer surgery patients. May be higher in patients with cachexia or significant metabolic stress. Adjust based on weight status and activity level.

💪 Protein Requirements

1.2–1.5 g/kg/day for cancer patients undergoing surgery (ESPEN recommendation). Higher protein intake (≥1.5 g/kg/day) may be warranted in patients with significant muscle wasting or severe malnutrition.

💊 Micronutrients

Address specific deficiencies identified on assessment (iron, vitamin D, B12, folate). Correct deficiencies before surgery where possible.

Intervention Strategies by Severity

Dietary Counselling

Individualised advice on food fortification, meal frequency, high-protein foods, and managing nutrition impact symptoms (nausea, early satiety, dysphagia).

Oral Nutritional Supplements (ONS)

High-protein, energy-dense supplements (e.g., 1.5–2.0 kcal/mL) prescribed when dietary intake is insufficient. Consider disease-specific formulas for GI cancers.

Enteral / Parenteral Nutrition

Reserved for patients with severe malnutrition or inability to meet requirements orally. Requires specialist dietitian and medical oversight. Consider 7–14 days preoperative nutrition support in severely malnourished patients.

8. Sarcopenia & Muscle Health

Low muscle mass (sarcopenia) is common in cancer patients and is independently associated with negative surgical outcomes including increased complications, prolonged hospital stay, and reduced survival. Sarcopenia may be present even in patients with normal or high BMI (sarcopenic obesity).

Assessing Muscle Mass

CT

CT Scan (L3 Skeletal Muscle Index)

Gold standard if available from staging CT. Measures muscle cross-sectional area at L3 vertebral level. No additional patient burden.

BIA

Bioelectrical Impedance Analysis (BIA)

Practical, non-invasive bedside tool. Requires specific validated equations for cancer patients. Affected by hydration status.

US

Muscle Ultrasound

Emerging bedside tool for measuring muscle thickness and quality. Requires trained operator.

↔

Surrogate Measures

Calf circumference and mid-upper arm circumference (MUAC) can be used when body composition equipment is unavailable.

Functional Muscle Assessment

✊

Handgrip Strength

Validated measure of muscle strength. Performed by physiotherapist, exercise physiologist, or dietitian. Predictive of surgical outcomes.

🚶

6-Minute Walk Test (6MWT)

Objective measure of functional capacity. Commonly used in prehabilitation programmes.

🪑

30-Second Sit-to-Stand

Measures lower limb strength and functional mobility. Easy to perform in clinic.

📊

ECOG Performance Status

Indirect measure of functional status. Useful for tracking change over the prehabilitation period.

Addressing Sarcopenia in Prehabilitation

Sarcopenia requires a combined nutrition and exercise approach. Nutrition alone is insufficient to reverse muscle loss without concurrent resistance training.

Nutrition Component

  • Adequate protein: ≥1.5 g/kg/day
  • Leucine-rich protein sources (dairy, legumes, eggs)
  • Protein distribution: 25–30 g per meal
  • Consider protein supplements if intake inadequate
  • Omega-3 fatty acids (EPA/DHA) may attenuate muscle loss

Exercise Component (via COACH)

  • Progressive resistance training: 2–3×/week
  • Target major muscle groups
  • Combine with aerobic training for functional gains
  • Physiotherapy-led, tailored to individual capacity

9. Perioperative Nutrition

Perioperative nutrition management extends beyond the prehabilitation period to include the immediate preoperative phase. ERAS (Enhanced Recovery After Surgery) protocols have transformed perioperative nutrition practice.

Preoperative Fasting — ERAS Guidance

6h

Solids — last solid food 6 hours before anaesthesia

2h

Clear fluids — allowed up to 2 hours before anaesthesia

CHO

Preoperative carbohydrate loading: 400 mL of 12.5% CHO drink night before, and 200 mL on morning of surgery (2h before), where not contraindicated

Immunonutrition

Immunonutrition formulas contain specific nutrients (arginine, omega-3 fatty acids, glutamine, nucleotides) that modulate the immune response and reduce inflammation. ESPEN guidelines recommend consideration in malnourished patients undergoing major cancer surgery.

⚡ ESPEN Recommendation

Peri-operative oral immunonutrition (arginine, omega-3 fatty acids, nucleotides) should be considered for 5–7 days before major cancer surgery, regardless of nutritional status. Discuss with dietitian and surgical team.

Postoperative Nutrition Principles

Early Oral Feeding

Resume oral intake as early as possible post-surgery (within hours if tolerated). Avoid prolonged nil-by-mouth periods.

Enteral Nutrition

Preferred over parenteral nutrition when gut function is present. Early enteral feeding (within 24h) reduces complications in major GI surgery.

Discharge Planning

Ensure ongoing nutrition support plan at discharge. ONS, home dietitian follow-up, and clear escalation pathways for weight loss or poor intake.

11. Clinical Decision Pathway — Nutritional Prehabilitation

Follow this step-by-step pathway for all patients referred to the prehabilitation service:

1

Screen ALL patients at first contact

Apply MST (preferred), MUST, or PG-SGA SF. Document score. All patients with cancer surgery planned should be screened regardless of apparent nutritional status.

2

Interpret screening score

MST ≥ 2 / MUST ≥ 2 / PG-SGA SF > 5 → At risk → Refer to dietitian. MST < 2 → Universal care (general nutrition advice, written resources).

3

Auto-refer high-risk surgical groups

All patients with major GI or gynaecological cancer surgery → Automatic dietitian referral regardless of screening score.

4

Dietitian: Comprehensive nutrition assessment (PG-SGA)

Complete full PG-SGA. Assess dietary history, anthropometrics, muscle mass, functional status, and nutrition impact symptoms. Apply GLIM criteria for malnutrition diagnosis.

5

Stratify: PG-SGA A / B / C

A (Well nourished): Universal care — general advice and written resources. B (Moderate): Targeted care — individualised counselling + ONS. C (Severe): Specialist care — intensive intervention, consider enteral/parenteral nutrition, consider delaying surgery.

6

Implement targeted intervention

Prescribe individualised nutrition plan. Address nutrition impact symptoms. Prescribe ONS if required. Refer to COACH for combined exercise and nutrition support where applicable.

7

Assess sarcopenia and muscle health

Assess muscle mass (CT, BIA, MUAC) and function (handgrip, 6MWT, sit-to-stand). Prescribe combined protein and resistance exercise programme for sarcopenic patients.

8

Perioperative nutrition planning

Ensure ERAS fasting protocol communicated. Prescribe preoperative carbohydrate loading. Consider immunonutrition for major GI cancer surgery. Plan early postoperative oral feeding.

9

Monitor and reassess

Repeat PG-SGA at each dietitian contact. Track weight, handgrip strength, and ONS compliance. Escalate to specialist care if status deteriorates. Reassess within 1–2 weeks of intervention commencement.

10

Discharge nutrition plan

Provide written discharge nutrition plan. Arrange community dietitian follow-up. Ensure GP is informed of nutritional status and ongoing needs. Escalation pathway for weight loss or poor intake post-discharge.

12. Summary

  • • Malnutrition is prevalent and modifiable — affecting 30–80% of cancer surgery patients depending on cancer type. It is directly linked to adverse surgical outcomes.
  • • Screen all patients using MST (preferred in Australia), MUST, or PG-SGA SF. Refer to dietitian for MST ≥ 2, MUST ≥ 2, or PG-SGA SF > 5.
  • • Comprehensive assessment (PG-SGA) by an accredited dietitian diagnoses malnutrition and stratifies patients into universal, targeted, or specialist care pathways.
  • • GLIM criteria provide a standardised, internationally recognised framework for malnutrition diagnosis using phenotypic and etiologic criteria.
  • • Protein targets of 1.2–1.5 g/kg/day are recommended for cancer surgery patients, with higher targets for those with sarcopenia or severe malnutrition.
  • • Sarcopenia requires combined nutrition and exercise — protein supplementation alone is insufficient without concurrent resistance training (refer to COACH).
  • • ERAS perioperative nutrition — 6-hour solid and 2-hour clear fluid fasting, preoperative carbohydrate loading, and early postoperative oral feeding are evidence-based standards.
  • • Immunonutrition should be considered for malnourished patients undergoing major cancer surgery (ESPEN recommendation).
