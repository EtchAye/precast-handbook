Exercise Prehabilitation

Exercise prehabilitation is a cornerstone of the PRECAST pathway. Poor preoperative functional capacity is a modifiable risk factor for postoperative complications, prolonged hospital stay, and delayed recovery. This chapter provides a comprehensive framework for exercise screening, functional assessment, and individualised exercise prescription for patients undergoing major cancer surgery.

Content is drawn from the Prep-4-Cancer Surgery Toolkit (Peter MacCallum Cancer Centre), the Exercise and Sports Science Australia (ESSA) position statement, and the American College of Sports Medicine (ACSM) international roundtable consensus on exercise for cancer survivors.

Learning Objectives

After completing this chapter, the clinician will be able to:

  1. 1Understand the rationale for exercise prehabilitation in cancer surgery pathways.
  2. 2Select and apply appropriate exercise screening tools to stratify patients by risk.
  3. 3Conduct or refer for comprehensive functional exercise assessment including CPET, 6MWT, ISWT, and sit-to-stand tests.
  4. 4Apply exercise prescription principles for aerobic and strength training in the prehabilitation context.
  5. 5Differentiate between targeted (moderate-risk) and specialist (high-risk/frail) exercise intervention pathways.
  6. 6Adapt exercise prescription for patients undergoing neoadjuvant therapy using a traffic-light system.
  7. 7Prescribe inspiratory muscle training (IMT) to reduce postoperative pulmonary complications.
  8. 8Plan appropriate discharge from the exercise prehabilitation service with a home exercise programme.

Key Recommendations

🏃 Universal Exercise Screening

All patients eligible for prehabilitation should undergo exercise screening to identify functional exercise capacity. DASI ≤34 or CFS ≥4 should trigger further assessment by a physiotherapist or exercise physiologist.

💪 Multimodal Exercise Prescription

Both aerobic and strength training should be included. Moderate-to-high intensity is appropriate for most patients. High-intensity interval training (HIIT) may be preferred when prehabilitation time is limited. Target 150 min/week of moderate-intensity or 75 min/week of vigorous exercise.

🫁 Inspiratory Muscle Training (IMT)

IMT should be considered for all high-risk surgical patients. Initiate at least 2 weeks before surgery at 30–50% of maximal inspiratory pressure (MIP), with weekly supervised sessions (face-to-face or telehealth).

📱 Telehealth Delivery

Telehealth is a feasible and effective delivery mode for exercise prehabilitation. Supervision via videoconferencing, web-based platforms, or exercise prescription apps (e.g. Telehab, Physitrack) is recommended where face-to-face is not possible.

1

Background & Clinical Rationale

Deconditioning is a potentially modifiable risk factor that significantly impacts postoperative recovery. Patients with poor preoperative physical function face higher rates of complications, longer hospital stays, and slower return to baseline activity. Exercise prehabilitation aims to reverse deconditioning and improve physiological reserve before surgery.

The brief period between cancer diagnosis and treatment is commonly perceived as a barrier to prehabilitation. However, evidence demonstrates that exercise prehabilitation of even two weeks or less can contribute meaningfully to enhanced postoperative recovery. Programmes of 2–8 weeks are most commonly used to achieve positive change in surgical risk profiles.

ESSA Position Statement Principles (Hayes et al., 2019)

  • → Exercise prescription must be individualised based on patient assessment, cancer-specific health issues, and patient-driven goals.
  • → While multimodal, moderate-to-high intensity exercise is appropriate for most patients, there is no single evidence-based prescription for all cancer patients.
  • → Targeted exercise prescription with behaviour change support is required to ensure greatest patient benefit with minimal risk of harm.
2

Evidence Base for Exercise Prehabilitation

Exercise prehabilitation is supported by a growing body of evidence across multiple cancer surgery populations. Key findings include reductions in postoperative pulmonary complications, shorter hospital length of stay, and improved functional recovery.

Exercise Screening

Who should be screened?

Exercise screening should be completed for all individuals eligible for prehabilitation. The aim is to identify the individual's level of functional exercise capacity and stratify them into universal, targeted, or specialist intervention pathways.

Why is screening important?

Poor functional exercise capacity is a risk factor for developing complications and poor postoperative recovery. Screening identifies patients who need further assessment and targeted or specialist intervention.

Distinguishing Physical Activity from Exercise

Physical Activity (PA)

"Any bodily movement produced by skeletal muscles that results in energy expenditure" (Caspersen et al., 1985)

Exercise

"Planned, structured, repetitive, and purposive in the sense that improvement or maintenance of one or more components of physical fitness is an objective" (Caspersen et al., 1985)

Both PA and exercise measurement are important in prehabilitation and should be considered separately in screening and assessment.

5

Functional Exercise Assessment

Individuals who are moderate or high risk on screening should be referred to a physiotherapist or exercise physiologist for comprehensive assessment. Those awaiting upper GI surgery (e.g. oesophagectomy, Whipple's) and complex abdominal surgery are at particularly high risk of deconditioning.

Subjective Assessment

  • • Social history: support network, home environment, employment, social deprivation indicators
  • • Exercise habits, available equipment, access to facilities
  • • Falls history
  • • Previous injuries or chronic musculoskeletal conditions
  • • Impact and control of comorbidities (respiratory, cardiac, endocrine, neurological)

Objective Assessment

  • • Establishes baseline physical capacity using formal outcome measures
  • • Allows risk stratification into targeted vs specialist pathways
  • • Provides measurable outcomes for monitoring progress
  • • Guides individualised exercise prescription
  • • Assessment template available from toolkit

Functional Assessment Measures

The following objective measures are recommended for comprehensive exercise assessment. All should be performed by a qualified physiotherapist or exercise physiologist.

Submaximal test of functional exercise capacity. Self-paced; primary outcome is distance walked. Two tests recommended for reproducibility (learning effect); one test is acceptable in clinical settings.

Exercise Prescription Principles

Exercise prescription should follow the FITT-VP framework (Frequency, Intensity, Time, Type, Volume, Progression), individualised to each patient's capacity, goals, and clinical context. Both aerobic and strength exercises can be delivered as continuous sets or interval training.

Rate of Perceived Exertion (RPE) Scale

The RPE scale (0–10) guides exercise dosage and progression. Target range is typically 4–6 (moderate to hard), but should be tailored to individual capacity.

8

Targeted Exercise Intervention (Moderate Risk)

Who receives targeted intervention?

Patients with DASI ≤34 on screening and, on further assessment:

  • • CPET: AT 11–15 mL/kg/min or VO₂ peak 15–20 mL/kg/min
  • • and/or 6MWT: 435–510 m

Delivery

  • • Physiotherapist or exercise physiologist
  • • Face-to-face, telehealth, or web-based delivery
  • • Twice per week supervised sessions (minimum)
  • • Home exercise programme between sessions

Review Frequency

  • • Fortnightly to monthly face-to-face or telehealth review
  • • Phone review if telehealth unavailable
  • • Review and progress home-based exercise programme
  • • Review goals at each contact

Note: Patients may transition between the stepped model of care. Intervention should be adjusted accordingly based on re-assessment findings.

9

Specialist Exercise Intervention (High Risk / Frail)

Who receives specialist intervention?

Patients who are more deconditioned or frail:

  • • CFS ≥5 (frail or severely frail)
  • • and/or 6MWT <435 m

Prescription Principles

  • • Follows same FITT-VP principles as targeted intervention
  • • Supervision may be required for all exercise sessions
  • • Face-to-face preferred for safety (e.g. falls risk)
  • • Lower starting intensities with careful progression

Review Frequency

  • • Weekly telehealth or face-to-face review
  • • Phone review if telehealth unavailable
  • • Some patients best managed face-to-face for safety
  • • Close monitoring for adverse events

Clinician Qualifications: Targeted and specialist exercise intervention should be delivered by physiotherapists or exercise physiologists familiar with the ESSA position statement and the Cancer Exercise Toolkit (cancerexercisetoolkit.trekeducation.org). For patients with bone metastases, follow the ESSA bone metastases consensus guidelines (2022).

Exercise Prescription During Neoadjuvant Therapy

Patients undergoing neoadjuvant therapy often experience fluctuating physical capacity due to symptom burden and treatment side effects. A flexible approach to exercise prescription is essential.

Traffic Light Self-Rating System

Ask the patient to self-rate how they are feeling that day before starting their programme. Adjust exercise intensity accordingly (Abo et al., 2021).

🟢

Green — Go

Feeling well. Proceed with full planned exercise programme at target intensity.

🟡

Amber — Modify

Feeling moderate. Reduce intensity and/or duration. Focus on gentle movement and breathing exercises.

🔴

Red — Rest

Feeling unwell. Rest or very gentle activity only. Do not push through significant fatigue or symptoms.

Chemotherapy Periodisation

Periodise exercise relative to chemotherapy/immunotherapy cycles (Kirkham et al., 2020):

Week of treatment administration

Reduce exercise time and frequency. Focus on gentle activity and breathing exercises. Monitor for adverse effects.

Between treatment cycles

Increase exercise frequency and intensity toward targets. Maximise training effect during recovery window.

11

Respiratory Exercises & Inspiratory Muscle Training (IMT)

All individuals preparing for surgery should receive universal respiratory education and breathing exercises. Inspiratory Muscle Training (IMT) should additionally be considered for all high-risk surgical patients.

What is IMT?

Repetitive progressive resistance training targeting the skeletal muscles responsible for inspiration. A threshold resistance device (held in the mouth) increases load on inspiratory muscles by resisting inspiratory airflow.

Evidence for IMT

  • • Halves risk of postoperative pulmonary complications in abdominal surgery (Kendall et al., 2018)
  • • Reduces hospital length of stay
  • • Benefits particularly notable in older and high-risk patients
  • • Most evidence in cardiac and thoracic surgery; growing evidence for abdominal surgery

IMT Protocol

ParameterRecommendation
TimingInitiate at least 2 weeks prior to surgery
Starting Intensity30–50% of maximal inspiratory pressure (MIP)
ProgressionProgressively increase resistance over time based on response
SupervisionWeekly supervised sessions (telehealth acceptable) for optimal outcomes
EquipmentThreshold resistance inspiratory muscle trainer (e.g. Threshold IMT device)

Discharge Planning from Exercise Prehabilitation

Most prehabilitation programmes run for 2–8 weeks. Discharge decisions should consider concurrent neoadjuvant treatment, patient progress, and adherence.

Criteria for Discharge

  • ✓ Patient is independent with exercise programme with consistent adherence
  • ✓ Achieved minimal clinically important difference (MCID) in appropriate outcome measures

Discharge Package

  • → Updated individualised home exercise programme (HEP)
  • → Contact details for prehabilitation clinician for patient queries
  • → Information on safe exercise: when to stop and who to contact
  • → ISBAR handover to inpatient physiotherapy team for continuity of care

ISBAR Handover: Ensure communication via the Identify, Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation (ISBAR) framework to the appropriate inpatient physiotherapy team at the time of surgery admission to ensure continuity of care.

Clinical Decision Pathway

Stepwise pathway for exercise prehabilitation from referral to discharge.

1

Referral & Eligibility

Patient referred to prehabilitation following cancer diagnosis. Confirm eligibility for exercise prehabilitation programme.

2

Universal Exercise Screening

Complete DASI/m-DASI and CFS for all patients. Add IPAQ-SF or GLTEQ for physical activity baseline. AKPS for performance status.

3

Risk Stratification

Low risk (DASI >34, CFS 1–3): universal intervention only. Moderate risk (DASI ≤34 or CFS 4–5): refer for targeted assessment. High risk (CFS ≥5): refer for specialist assessment.

4

Functional Assessment (Moderate/High Risk)

Physiotherapist or EP conducts subjective and objective assessment. Select from: 6MWT, ISWT, CPET, 30STS, TUG, SPPB, hand grip, pelvic floor questionnaire as appropriate.

5

Exercise Prescription

Individualise aerobic and strength programme. Apply FITT-VP principles. Set RPE targets. Include IMT for high-risk surgical patients. Document prescription and goals.

6

Delivery & Supervision

Targeted: 2×/week supervised sessions (face-to-face or telehealth) + home programme. Specialist: weekly or more frequent supervision; face-to-face if falls risk.

7

Neoadjuvant Adaptation

Apply traffic-light self-rating system. Periodise exercise around chemotherapy cycles (reduce in treatment week, increase between cycles).

8

Review & Progress

Fortnightly–monthly (targeted) or weekly (specialist) review. Re-measure outcome measures. Adjust prescription. Reassess stepped care level if needed.

9

Discharge Planning

Confirm MCID achieved and patient independence with HEP. Provide discharge package. Complete ISBAR handover to inpatient physiotherapy team.

10

Surgery

Patient proceeds to surgery with optimised functional capacity and postoperative physiotherapy plan in place.
