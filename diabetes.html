Chapter Overview

Chapter Number: 6
Last Updated: May 2026
Target Audience: Health professionals involved in cancer surgery prehabilitation programs, including surgeons, anaesthetists, nurses, allied health professionals, and general practitioners.

Learning Objectives

By the end of this chapter, the reader will be able to:

  1. Identify the types of diabetes mellitus relevant to the oncology setting
  2. Determine which patients require preoperative diabetes screening
  3. Order and interpret appropriate investigations for diabetes assessment
  4. Apply diagnostic criteria for diabetes mellitus using HbA1c and glucose measurements
  5. Assess preoperative glycaemic control and identify optimal targets
  6. Manage diabetes medications appropriately in the preoperative period
  7. Recognise and manage newly diagnosed diabetes in cancer surgery patients
  8. Implement quality improvement measures to evaluate diabetes management programs

⚡ Key Clinical Recommendations

Critical Actions

  • •Screen all at-risk patients with HbA1c (known DM, cardiovascular risk factors, BMI >25, age >40, glucocorticoid use, family history)
  • •Optimal glycaemic target: HbA1c ≤6.5% before surgery
  • •High-risk threshold: HbA1c ≥10% is associated with postoperative complications
  • •Withhold diabetes medications at appropriate intervals before fasting to avoid hypoglycaemia, acidosis, and aspiration
  • •Refer newly diagnosed T2D to GP for dietary measures and oral hypoglycaemic agents
  • •Refer to endocrinology: Patients requiring insulin therapy or blood glucose >16 mmol/L when surgery cannot be delayed

1. Introduction and Clinical Context

Diabetes mellitus (DM) is a common metabolic disorder characterised by chronic hyperglycaemia resulting from defects in insulin secretion, insulin action, or both. In the context of cancer surgery prehabilitation, diabetes represents a significant modifiable risk factor that requires systematic identification and optimisation.

Poor glycaemic control in the perioperative period is associated with substantial morbidity, including increased infection rates, impaired wound healing, prolonged hospital stay, and elevated mortality risk. Beyond surgical outcomes, suboptimal diabetes management in cancer patients has broader implications for oncological care, including:

  • Reduced efficacy of chemotherapy
  • Increased adverse effects from therapy
  • Requirement for treatment modifications
  • Increased emergency department visits

The prevalence of diabetes in the surgical oncology population is substantial, affecting more than 1 in 20 Australians overall, with rates increasing to 1 in 5 in older Australians. Additionally, undiagnosed diabetes is common, with prevalence rates of approximately 16% identified in Australian general practice screening studies.

Poor glycaemic control is independently associated with increased surgical site infections, anastomotic leaks, cardiovascular events, and 30-day mortality. The relationship between diabetes and oncological outcomes is bidirectional — cancer and its treatment can worsen glycaemic control, while poor glycaemic control can compromise treatment efficacy.

4. Patient Selection and Screening

Who Should Be Screened

At-risk patients requiring HbA1c screening:

✓ Known diabetes mellitus (any type)
✓ Cardiovascular risk factors
✓ BMI >25 kg/m²
✓ Age >40 years
✓ Glucocorticoid use
✓ Family history of diabetes
✓ Previous gestational diabetes
✓ Polycystic ovarian syndrome (PCOS)

Screening should be performed at the prehabilitation assessment, ideally 4–6 weeks before surgery to allow time for optimisation. Universal HbA1c testing may be considered in cancer surgery populations given the high prevalence of undiagnosed diabetes.

💡 HbA1c Limitations in Cancer Patients

HbA1c may be unreliable in patients with haemolytic anaemia, haemoglobinopathies, recent blood transfusion, iron deficiency, or renal failure. In these cases, fasting glucose or OGTT may be more appropriate.

6. Preoperative Assessment and Glycaemic Targets

≤6.5%

Optimal HbA1c

Target before elective surgery

6.5–10%

Acceptable Range

Optimise where possible; proceed with caution

≥10%

High Risk

Associated with postoperative complications

Preoperative assessment should include review of current diabetes management, identification of diabetes complications (nephropathy, neuropathy, retinopathy, cardiovascular disease), and assessment of hypoglycaemia risk. The optimal HbA1c target of ≤6.5% may not be achievable in all patients — clinical judgment is required, balancing the risks of poor glycaemic control against the risks of intensive therapy (particularly hypoglycaemia).

7. Medication Management in the Preoperative Period

Careful preoperative management of diabetes medications is essential to avoid hypoglycaemia, ketoacidosis, lactic acidosis, and aspiration risk.

Medication ClassExamplesPreoperative ManagementRationale
MetforminMetforminWithhold on day of surgeryRisk of lactic acidosis; hold if contrast or renal impairment
SulfonylureasGlipizide, gliclazideWithhold on day of surgeryRisk of hypoglycaemia during fasting
SGLT2 inhibitorsEmpagliflozin, dapagliflozin, canagliflozinWithhold 3–4 days before surgeryRisk of euglycaemic DKA (eDKA) — even with normal glucose
GLP-1 receptor agonistsSemaglutide, liraglutideWithhold on day of surgery (weekly agents: withhold 1 week prior)Delayed gastric emptying increases aspiration risk
DPP-4 inhibitorsSitagliptin, saxagliptinWithhold on day of surgeryLow hypoglycaemia risk but withhold as precaution during fasting
Basal insulinGlargine, detemir, degludecReduce dose by 20% on day before and day of surgeryMust never be completely withheld — prevents DKA (especially T1D)
Prandial (bolus) insulinAspart, lispro, glulisineWithhold on day of surgery (no meals = no bolus)Hypoglycaemia risk during fasting
Mixed insulinNovomix, Humalog MixWithhold on day of surgery; convert to basal-bolus regimenRequires specialist input for perioperative management

⚠ SGLT2 Inhibitor Warning

SGLT2 inhibitors must be withheld 3–4 days before surgery due to the risk of euglycaemic diabetic ketoacidosis (eDKA). This can occur even when blood glucose appears normal, making it particularly dangerous in the perioperative period.

Type 1 Diabetes — Special Consideration

Basal insulin must never be completely withheld in Type 1 diabetes. Even during fasting, basal insulin is required to prevent diabetic ketoacidosis. Refer to endocrinology for perioperative insulin management in all T1D patients.

8. Management of Newly Diagnosed Diabetes

9. Quality Improvement and Evaluation

Epidemiological Metrics

  • Prevalence of DM in screened population
  • Prevalence of undiagnosed DM
  • Prevalence of suboptimal glycaemic control (HbA1c ≥10%)

Process Metrics

  • Screening rate (HbA1c in at-risk patients)
  • Compliance with medication withholding protocols
  • Endocrinology referral rate for high-risk patients
  • Timing of HbA1c relative to surgery

Efficacy Metrics

  • Mean HbA1c change from prehabilitation to surgery
  • Proportion achieving HbA1c ≤6.5%
  • Rate of perioperative hypoglycaemia
  • Rate of perioperative hyperglycaemia

Perioperative Outcome Metrics

  • Surgical site infection rate
  • Anastomotic leak rate
  • Hospital length of stay
  • 30-day readmission rate

10. Clinical Decision Pathway

1

Patient Identification

Identify at-risk patients at prehabilitation assessment: known DM, BMI >25, age >40, cardiovascular risk factors, glucocorticoid use, family history.

2

Order Screening Investigations

Order HbA1c (4–6 weeks before surgery). Consider fasting glucose or OGTT if HbA1c unreliable (haemolytic anaemia, haemoglobinopathy, recent transfusion).

3

Interpret Results

HbA1c <6.5% → No DM (or well-controlled existing DM). HbA1c 6.5–10% → DM diagnosed/known, optimise. HbA1c ≥10% → High-risk, refer to endocrinology.

4

Newly Diagnosed DM

If newly diagnosed T2D → Refer to GP for dietary measures and oral hypoglycaemics. If insulin required or blood glucose >16 mmol/L → Refer to endocrinology.

5

Review Diabetes Medications

Identify all diabetes medications. Develop preoperative withholding plan. Withhold SGLT2 inhibitors 3–4 days before surgery. Withhold other agents on day of surgery. Never completely withhold basal insulin in T1D.

6

Patient Education

Provide written medication withholding instructions. Educate on hypoglycaemia symptoms and management. Advise on blood glucose monitoring during fasting period.

7

Perioperative Monitoring Plan

Establish blood glucose monitoring protocol for perioperative period. Communicate DM status and management plan to surgical and anaesthetic teams.

8

Proceed to Surgery

Confirm HbA1c target achieved or documented reason for proceeding. Confirm medication withholding instructions communicated. Confirm perioperative monitoring plan in place.

9

Postoperative Follow-up

Resume diabetes medications as appropriate postoperatively. Arrange follow-up with GP or endocrinology. Consider long-term diabetes prevention for patients with pre-diabetes identified at screening.

11. Summary and Key Points

  1. Diabetes is common in cancer surgery patients — affecting 1 in 20 Australians overall, with higher rates in older patients and high rates of undiagnosed disease.
  2. Systematic preoperative screening with HbA1c should be performed in all at-risk patients 4–6 weeks before surgery.
  3. The optimal glycaemic target is HbA1c ≤6.5% before elective surgery; HbA1c ≥10% is associated with significant postoperative complications.
  4. Multiple types of diabetes are relevant in the oncology setting, including glucocorticoid-induced DM and immune checkpoint inhibitor-related T1D.
  5. HbA1c has limitations in some cancer patients — consider fasting glucose or OGTT when HbA1c is unreliable.
  6. All diabetes medications require careful preoperative management to avoid hypoglycaemia, ketoacidosis, lactic acidosis, and aspiration.
  7. SGLT2 inhibitors must be withheld 3–4 days before surgery due to eDKA risk — even when blood glucose appears normal.
  8. Type 1 diabetes requires specialist management — basal insulin must never be completely withheld.
  9. Newly diagnosed T2D should be referred to GP; patients requiring insulin or with blood glucose >16 mmol/L should be referred to endocrinology.
  10. Preoperative screening provides an opportunity for case-finding and long-term diabetes prevention beyond the surgical episode.
